Anticipatory governance

The intellectual ancestor of anticipatory governance is Alvin Toffler's notion of anticipatory democracy, which for Toffler means citizen participation coupled with future consciousness \cite{bezold_anticipatory_1978}.

Anticipatory governance of emerging technologies

The term "anticipatory governance" is widely in use in the field of Science and Technology Studies (STS), particularly with relation to nanotechnology. Guston defines anticipatory governance as a "broad-based capacity extended through society that can act on a variety oof inputs to manage emerging knowledge-based technologies while such management is still possible" \cite{Guston_2014}. The starting point is the so-called Collingridge dilemma: first the social consequences of emerging technologies are unclear, and when they become clear, it is already too late to act on them due to path-dependence and technological lock-in.
Anticipatory governance aims to build capacities in foresight, engagement and integration \cite{Guston_2014}.
In the early 2000s, two strands of literature in anticipatory governance began to emerge: 1) public administration and management, and 2) environmental studies and policy \cite{Guston_2014}.
Critics have pointed out that the perspective of anticipatory governance may contribute to the hubris of technoscience \cite{Boenink_2013,Guston_2014}.
